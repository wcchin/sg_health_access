Health-care Accessibility in Singapore

A pilot study for building the model

Motivation

As one of the most densely populated countries in the world, Singapore faces unique challenges in ensuring equal access to healthcare for all. With nearly 6 million people living on an area smaller than New York City, transportation and crowdedness can be a major barrier to accessing clinics. The high density of population has led to an increased demand for healthcare services, putting strain on the country's medical infrastructure.

Therefore, although clinics are located in every neighborhood, the quality and intensity of healthcare services could vary between people living in different parts of the city state, leading to disparities in access. This project seeks to address these issues by encouraging creative solutions to measure the availability of clinics and the intensity of the healthcare services throughout the country. In this project, we present a framework to measure the healthcare intensity by combining two popular models: radiation models and 2-step floating catchment area (2SFCA).

Methodology

Distance estimation: Dijkstra algorithm

Before the estimation of flows using radiation model, the travel distances between every pair of spatial units (hexagon cells) are needed. To do this, OSMNX package (a Python package) was used for the routing. OpenStreetMap's road networks (driving) were used in OSMNX. Dijkstra algorithm was used in OSMNX, which returned the shortest path between two locations (the centroid of hexagons) traveling on the specified road network. The travel distances between all pairs of hexagon cells were used to generate a complete graph, that indicated the cost of travels.

Flow estimation: Radiation model

Radiation model compare the services at the targeted locations with the aggregated services within the nearby areas, i.e., with travel distance shorter than the targeted locations. Here, the travel distance is based on the complete graph generated in step 1. The radiation model equation (as in Simini et al. (2012) paper) is shown below. The equation is modified to match with our model setting, e.g., \(n_j\) is the number of clinics in a cell and \(m_i\) is set to 1. $${\langle T_{ij} \rangle = T_i \times \frac{m_i n_j}{(m_i+s_{ij})(m_i+n_j+s_{ij})}}$$ $${s_{ij}=\sum_{k \in D_{ij}}n_k}$$ $${D_{ij}=\text{spatial units with a distance shorter than }dist_{ij}}$$

Service intensity: 2-Step Floating Catchment Area

2SFCA is used for estimating accessibility of a service with the consideration of the service-to-population ratio. In simple words, if the service area of a clinic covered a large number of people, the ratio would be low, which indicates that a lower intensity (poorer cares) could be assigned to every person within the service area. Therefore, the first step of 2SFCA is to calculate the ratio (as in Luo and Wang (2003) paper): $${R_j = \frac{S_j}{\sum_{k\in SA_j} P_k }}$$ $${SA_j = \text{spatial units in service area of } j}$$ A distance threshold parameter is needed to define service area. The second step is to sum up the ratios of spatial units within a service distance for each origin. The equations were modified to match the condition of our model. The resulting index is used to capture the service intensity of every hexagon cells that it received. $${A_i = \sum_{j \in S_i} R_j}$$ $${S_i = \text{destination within the distance threshold}}$$
